# Spike 001 — Language-Learning App Landscape & Paid-Product Read

**Date:** June 2026 · **For:** MultiGlot / mynewwords.org — a solo (NL-based) indie product: a personal-vocabulary FSRS spaced-repetition drill with AI-generated decks, a multi-language "weave," audio→vocab (your own recording → auto-extracted deck), IPA/etymology/mnemonics, and a "drill YOUR OWN words from real life" ethos. Weighing whether to go **paid**.

**Scope:** ~30 apps/tools across four clusters, plus market & monetization dynamics. Time-boxed breadth spike (web search + fetch), not exhaustive.

**Honesty note — pricing drifts fast.** Every price below is dated to the June 2026 research pass and tagged where it could not be verified from a primary/official source. Many curriculum apps (Babbel, Mondly, Rosetta Stone, Memrise) are *near-permanently on sale* — the "discount" price is effectively the real price, the MSRP is theatre. App-store prices typically run ~30% above web prices (Apple/Google fees). Treat one-time/"lifetime" figures from big vendors as promo-anchored, not stable.

A companion feature-matrix (7 differentiator axes, per-app) lives at `docs/competitive-analysis.md` — this spike is the broader landscape + pricing + monetization layer on top of it.

**Theme finding — AI deck-gen is now table-stakes-but-rented.** Quizlet (Magic Notes), Brainscape (Copilot), plus Laxu ($4.99/mo), RemNote (~$8–18/mo), StudyFetch ($19/mo), Turbo AI ($19.99/mo), Ankify (one-time after 100 free cards) all generate decks from notes/PDFs. **Every one is subscription-gated $5–$20/mo; none is one-time** (except Ankify). A polished, low-friction, or self-hosted-cost AI gen stands out only on *quality + UX + price model*, not on existence.

**Theme finding — FSRS is a credible-modern signal.** Only the real Anki ecosystem ships FSRS. Every commercial competitor (Quizlet, Brainscape, Vocabulary.com, Drops, AnkiApp, Lingvist) runs a *proprietary or lighter* adaptive scheme. (Honesty flag: Anki's own FAQ still lists SM-2 as the default as of this pass; the maintainer's Dec-2024 plan to make FSRS the out-of-box default was implemented in a 2025 release, but the exact version that flipped the default for new collections is unconfirmed.) MultiGlot building on FSRS is a genuine "we use the modern engine" differentiator versus the polished-but-shallow crowd.

## Analysis

### 1. Segmentation map — where MultiGlot sits

Five real clusters, by *who authors the content* and *what the unit of study is*:

1. **Gamified curriculum** (Duolingo, Mondly, Memrise) — vendor-authored lessons, habit loop, free-tier-driven. Mass market.
2. **Structured curriculum** (Babbel, Busuu, LingoDeer, Rosetta Stone, Pimsleur) — vendor-authored, pedagogy-serious, premium-priced.
3. **Adaptive vocab supplements** (Lingvist, Speakly, Vocabulary.com, Drops) — vendor-authored *vocabulary* with adaptive scheduling. Vocab-only, fixed content.
4. **Custom-vocab / SRS** (Anki, Quizlet, Brainscape) — **user-authored** decks, scheduling engine. The "your own words" half — but you type them, and they're monolingual-pair by convention.
5. **Your-own-content / comprehensible input** (LingQ, Readlang, Clozemaster, Migaku, Language Reactor) — you bring *content* (text/media); the app extracts words. The "real life" half — but content-in is usually text/streaming, and output is a *lesson*, not a clean deck.

**MultiGlot sits at the intersection of clusters 4 and 5** that nobody occupies cleanly: **user-authored vocabulary (cluster 4's "your own words") fed by your own real-life content including audio (cluster 5's ingest), output as a clean FSRS deck with language pedagogy (IPA/etymology/mnemonics) across a multi-language weave.** Anki has the SRS but not the ingest or the pedagogy auto-fill; LingQ/Migaku have the ingest but output lessons and aren't private; the vocab supplements have polish but vendor-fixed content.

### 2. White space — what's genuinely uncommon

| MultiGlot angle | Verdict | Closest analog |
|---|---|---|
| **Audio→vocab from your OWN recording → clean deck** | **Genuinely uncommon.** No dominant polished consumer product. | **VoiceLingua** (dedicated, but real-time-capture, unclear file-upload, unverified privacy/pricing). **LingQ** + **Migaku** (Apr 2026) do own-audio→Whisper but output a *lesson/clips*, not a tidy deck. **AnkiDecks** does the mechanic but isn't language-aware. |

**The clearest, most defensible white space:** **audio→vocab from your own recordings, privacy-first, output as a clean FSRS deck with auto-filled pedagogy (IPA/etymology/mnemonic) across a multi-language weave.** No single competitor combines even three of those. The audio→vocab + privacy + clean-deck triple is the sharpest wedge; VoiceLingua is the only thing close and it's thin/unverified.

### 3. Monetization — what users actually pay for here

**The honest funnel math (RevenueCat 2026, 115k-app dataset — the most credible indie benchmark):**
- Education **download→paid ≈ 2.3% median** (high-priced apps ~2.8%, low-priced ~1.4% — counter-intuitively, higher price → higher conversion, because better-qualified buyers).
- Education **download→trial ≈ 6.5%**, but only ~a third of trials convert.
- **Year-one revenue per payer ≈ $22.82 median.**
- Education has **strong weekly retention (~58%)** but **weak annual renewal** — people learn for a goal then churn. **SRS is one of the few sub-niches with a structural retention edge**, because spaced repetition is inherently a long-horizon habit, not a one-trip goal. That's the single best argument for a recurring price in this space.
- Duolingo converts **~5% of MAUs** to paid and grows that ~1pt/year by *adding value to the paid tier*, not squeezing free.

**Subscription vs one-time:**
- **Subscription is the category default and where ~all reported revenue is** (Duolingo subscription bookings ~$996M FY2025). The empirical center of gravity (RevenueCat education medians): **$9.99/mo, $39.99/yr.**
- **One-time is the Anki outlier: AnkiMobile $24.99 once** (verified) — but that works *because Anki has zero per-user variable cost* (no LLM, no server-side transcription) and a decade of free-tier network effects. It's a survivorship outlier, not a template.
- **MultiGlot has real per-user variable cost** (LLM deck-gen + transcription compute). A lifetime buyer who generates 500 decks costs money forever. That structurally argues *against* pure one-time.
- The repeated indie consensus: **hybrid — one-time/cheap core, subscription (or credits) for the cost-bearing extras.**

**The "free pressure" reality:**
- Anki owns free SRS at $0 marginal cost; Duolingo has a strong free tier. **You cannot win on "vocabulary SRS" as a commodity — its market price is zero.** Most users won't pay; ~2–3% will, and they'll pay for **convenience + the AI/audio features that cost you money**, not for SRS itself.
- The well-attested indie lesson: **free-tier-first beats paid-first.** A niche-AI dev who launched paid-trial-first to "validate willingness to pay" found a free tier was *"the single biggest growth lever they pulled"* — "willingness to pay is what investor decks are for; PMF is what free tiers are for."
- The moat vs. free is exactly MultiGlot's distinctive features: **AI deck-gen (Anki needs a plugin + your own key + setup), audio→vocab (no free incumbent does it), and a humane UI** (Anki's UX is famously hostile — the entire "Anki alternative" cluster exists on this wedge).

**AI cost reality (so it doesn't drive fear):**
- Claude Haiku 4.5 = $1/$5 per 1M in/out tokens; with caching/batch, less. One deck-gen ≈ **~$0.02**. A heavy user (50 decks/mo) ≈ **~$1/mo** in LLM cost.
- MultiGlot **self-hosts whisper.cpp** → transcription marginal cost ≈ **~$0** at indie scale (self-host break-even vs API is ~2,400–3,000 hrs/mo; you're far below).
- So a **$3–5/mo** sub covers a typical user's AI cost with healthy margin. **AI cost is not the constraint — abuse is.** Cap generation per period; reserve BYO-key as an optional power-user escape hatch (it kills conversion as a default).
- How AI apps price around variable cost: **quota-inside-subscription** (RemNote 100 credits/mo free → $18/mo full AI; Mindomax 3/day → 90/day on $5/mo) is the dominant, predictable pattern.

### 4. Recommendation if going paid

**Go paid — but freemium-hybrid, not pure-paid and not pure-one-time.**

- **Positioning:** *"Drill the words from your own life — your voicemails, your reading, your three languages at once — with the modern SRS engine and zero Anki pain."* Lead with **audio→vocab + privacy + your-own-words**, not "another SRS app." That's the white space; SRS and AI-gen are the supporting cast.
- **Model — hybrid freemium:**
  - **Free core (PMF + goodwill, competes with Anki on UX):** unlimited manual vocab, FSRS scheduling, the multi-language weave, IPA toggle, your-own-data ownership/export. Give away the commodity.
  - **Paid (the differentiated, cost-bearing parts):** AI deck-generation, audio→vocab (transcription), etymology/mnemonic auto-fill — with a **generous monthly quota** (quota-inside-subscription, RemNote/Mindomax pattern). Cap generation to bound cost/abuse, not to nickel-and-dime.
- **Price (rough, 2026 band):**
  - **Subscription anchor: ~$39.99/yr (≈€35–39) or ~$4–5/mo.** That's the RevenueCat education median *and* sits under every incumbent — credible "more focused, less than Babbel" story. Don't exceed ~$50/yr without a strong AI hook; an unknown solo app can't credibly charge $90/yr.
  - **Optional one-time base (~$25–35) for Anki-crowd goodwill**, with AI/audio as a separate subscription or credit pack — only if you want the bootstrapped-hacker audience. Cleaner to start subscription-only with a free tier.
- **Free-vs-paid split (concrete):** Free = manual decks + FSRS + weave + IPA + export. Paid = AI gen + audio→vocab + auto-pedagogy, metered by a monthly quota with a power-user/BYO-key escape hatch.
- **Sequencing:** Ship the **free tier first** to find PMF and earn the Anki-refugee goodwill; turn on paid once the AI/audio features are the obvious thing people would pay to not set up themselves. Expect **~2–3% of users to pay, ~$23/payer year one** — plan the funnel around that, not optimism.

**Reasoned caveat:** the niche is small and the free incumbents are strong, so willingness-to-pay is thin. The bet only works if the **audio→vocab + privacy + humane-UX** wedge is genuinely better than DIY-Anki-plus-a-plugin — that's the product risk, not the pricing risk.
